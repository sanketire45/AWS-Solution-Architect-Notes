CloudFront · Part 1

The Problem CloudFront Solves

Why does a web request from Sydney to a server in Dublin feel slow — and how do you fix it without buying servers in Sydney?

Imagine your website's servers live in one building in Dublin, Ireland. A user in Sydney, Australia clicks a link. What actually happens?

Their request has to physically travel ~17,000 km across undersea cables to Dublin, your server processes it, and the response travels 17,000 km back. Light is fast, but not instant — each round trip eats 250–300 milliseconds just in travel time. A single web page makes dozens of these trips (HTML, then CSS, images, JavaScript, fonts…). Those milliseconds stack up into seconds of waiting, and a slow page loses customers.

Now multiply that by everyone. Every image, every video, every download — your one Dublin server handles every request from every human on Earth. It melts under load, and far-away users get a sluggish experience no matter how fast your code is.

You could buy and run servers in every country. But now you're back in the nightmare S3 saved you from: buying hardware, guessing capacity, patching machines, copying files everywhere. There has to be a better way.

💡 Why CloudFront Was Built

CloudFront is a CDN — a Content Delivery Network. The idea: instead of every user reaching your one origin server, AWS keeps copies of your content in hundreds of small data centres scattered around the world (called edge locations or PoPs — Points of Presence). A user in Sydney is served from a Sydney edge location ~5 ms away, not from Dublin 300 ms away.

The first time content is requested in a region, CloudFront fetches it from your origin once, caches it at the nearby edge, and serves every subsequent user from that local copy. Your origin server suddenly handles a tiny fraction of the traffic, and users everywhere get near-instant responses. You manage one origin; AWS handles the global distribution.

CloudFront · Part 2

How CloudFront Works — The Mental Model

Edge locations, the cache hit/miss flow, and regional edge caches.

The vocabulary, before the jargon hits

  • Origin — where your real content lives. An S3 bucket, an Application Load Balancer, an EC2 instance, or any HTTP server (even outside AWS).
  • Edge Location (PoP) — one of 600+ small AWS sites worldwide that cache and serve content close to users. This is what makes it fast.
  • Distribution — your CloudFront configuration. It gives you a domain like d111abc.cloudfront.net (you point your real domain at it).
  • Cache Hit — the edge already has the content → serves instantly, never touches your origin.
  • Cache Miss — the edge doesn't have it (yet) → fetches from origin once, caches it, then serves.
  • TTL (Time To Live) — how long the edge keeps a cached copy before checking the origin again.
First Sydney user = cache miss (one trip to Dublin). Every Sydney user after = cache hit (~5 ms, origin never touched).
🔑 Regional Edge Caches — the layer in between

Between the small edge locations and your origin sits a second, larger caching tier: Regional Edge Caches. When content is evicted from a small edge (they have limited space), CloudFront checks the bigger regional cache before going all the way back to your origin. This keeps less-popular content warm and further reduces origin load. You don't configure it — it's automatic.

💰 A CloudFront distribution with zero traffic costs $0 — there's no hourly fee. You pay only per-GB delivered and per-request. And the first 1 TB out + 10M requests every month are free forever (not just year one). (Contrast this with Global Accelerator later, which has a fixed ~$18/month floor.)

CloudFront · Part 3

Origins & Locking Them Down — OAC vs OAI

How to connect CloudFront to S3 and make sure users can only reach your bucket through CloudFront.

CloudFront can sit in front of several origin types:

Origin typeUse it forNote
S3 bucket (REST endpoint)Static files: images, video, JS, CSS, downloadsLock to CloudFront with OAC
S3 static website endpointS3-hosted website with redirects/index docsTreated as a custom origin (HTTP only)
ALB / EC2 / custom HTTPDynamic apps, APIs, anything that generates pagesOrigin can even be on-prem / non-AWS
Media services / MediaPackageLive & on-demand video streaming—

The classic problem: if your S3 bucket is public so CloudFront can read it, then anyone who finds the raw bucket URL bypasses CloudFront entirely — skipping your caching, logging, WAF, and signed-URL protection.

The fix: keep the bucket private, and grant access to only CloudFront. This is what OAC (Origin Access Control) does. CloudFront signs its requests to S3; a bucket policy allows that CloudFront distribution and denies everyone else.

⚠️ Exam Trap — OAC vs OAI (this WILL appear)

OAI (Origin Access Identity) is the old, legacy way to lock S3 to CloudFront. AWS now says use OAC (Origin Access Control) instead. The single most tested difference:

OAI cannot decrypt SSE-KMS objects. OAC can. If a question mentions S3 objects encrypted with SSE-KMS behind CloudFront → the answer is OAC, and OAI is the trap distractor. OAC also supports all AWS Regions, dynamic requests (POST/PUT), and is the AWS-recommended default. When both OAC and OAI appear as options, pick OAC.

🔑 Origin Groups — origin failover

You can configure a primary + secondary origin as an Origin Group. If the primary returns a failure status you specify (e.g. 500, 502, 503, 504, or 403/404), CloudFront automatically retries the secondary. This is CloudFront-level high availability for your origin — faster and simpler than DNS failover. Caveat: origin failover only applies to GET/HEAD/OPTIONS — not transactional writes.

CloudFront · Part 4

Caching, TTLs & Invalidations

The double-edged sword: caching makes you fast, but serves stale content if you're not careful.

How long does an edge keep a copy? That's the TTL, controlled by Cache Policies (and origin Cache-Control headers). Short TTL = fresher content but more origin trips; long TTL = faster & cheaper but riskier staleness.

Cache keys decide what counts as "the same" object. By default that's the URL path, but you can include query strings, headers, or cookies in the key — useful when the same URL returns different content (e.g. ?lang=fr).

⚠️ Classic Trap — stale content after deploy

You update logo.png at your origin, but users still see the old logo. Why? The edge is still serving its cached copy until the TTL expires. Two fixes: (1) create a cache invalidation — explicitly tell CloudFront to purge /logo.png from all edges; or (2, better) use versioned file names (logo.v2.png) so new content has a new URL and never collides with the cache.

💰 Invalidations: the first 1,000 paths/month are free, then $0.005 per path. Purging thousands of files on every deploy adds up — which is exactly why versioned filenames (free) are the architect's preferred pattern over constant invalidations.
CloudFront · Part 5

Security — HTTPS, Signed URLs & Access Control

Serving private/paid content, enforcing HTTPS, restricting by country, and blocking attacks.

HTTPS & the us-east-1 certificate trap

CloudFront gives you free HTTPS on its default domain. For your own domain (cdn.myapp.com) you attach a TLS certificate from ACM (AWS Certificate Manager).

⚠️ Heavily Tested Trap

An ACM certificate used with CloudFront MUST be created in the us-east-1 (N. Virginia) region — no matter where your users or origin are. (For an ALB, the cert lives in the same region as the ALB.) A cert in the wrong region is a frequent distractor. Remember: CloudFront cert → us-east-1, always.

Signed URLs vs Signed Cookies — serving private content

For paid or private content (a premium video, a paywalled PDF), you don't want public URLs. CloudFront signed URLs and signed cookies grant time-limited access:

Signed URL

One file at a time

  • Grants access to a single file
  • Each file needs its own signed URL
  • Use for: a one-off download, a single video
  • Works even where cookies aren't supported
Signed Cookies

Many files at once

  • Grants access to multiple files / whole sections
  • One cookie unlocks a subscriber area or an HLS video (many segments)
  • Use for: "premium member accessing many videos"
  • No need to change your URLs
🔑 Don't confuse with S3 Presigned URLs

S3 presigned URL = temporary access to upload or download directly from S3 (often for letting a user upload without AWS credentials). CloudFront signed URL/cookie = temporary access to content delivered through the CDN (with caching + edge protection). Trigger words: "upload to S3 with no credentials" → S3 presigned; "distribute premium media globally with access control + caching" → CloudFront signed URL/cookie.

Geo-restriction, WAF & field-level encryption

  • Geo-restriction — allowlist or blocklist entire countries (e.g. licensing rules: "this content only in the EU"). CloudFront uses a geo-IP database to enforce it at the edge.
  • AWS WAF — attach a Web ACL to your distribution to block SQL injection, rate-limit abusive IPs, filter bots. The standard global-app security pattern is CloudFront + WAF + Shield.
  • Field-level encryption — encrypt specific sensitive fields (e.g. a credit-card number) at the edge with a public key, so only the specific backend with the private key can decrypt them. The data stays encrypted as it passes through your stack.
  • Shield Standard is automatic & free on CloudFront (DDoS protection at the edge); Shield Advanced adds cost protection + the response team.
CloudFront · Part 6

Edge Compute — CloudFront Functions vs Lambda@Edge

Run code at the edge to customise requests/responses without a round trip to your origin.

Sometimes you want to run a little logic at the edge — rewrite a header, redirect based on a cookie, do A/B testing, add security headers, or generate a response. Two tools do this, and the exam loves making you pick between them.

CloudFront FunctionsLambda@Edge
LanguageJavaScript onlyNode.js / Python

TriggersViewer request / response onlyAll 4: viewer + origin request/response

🔑 Decision rule

"Simple, fast, cheap header/URL rewrite at huge scale" → CloudFront Functions. "Needs a network/SDK call, heavier logic, or runs at the origin" → Lambda@Edge. Picking Lambda@Edge where a CloudFront Function would do is the "over-engineered / costlier" wrong answer. (Note: Lambda@Edge functions are authored in us-east-1 and replicated to edges.)

How an architect reasons about the CDN bill — and why it usually makes egress cheaper, not dearer.

💰 The lever most people miss

Serving content through CloudFront is usually cheaper than serving it straight from S3 or EC2, for two reasons: (1) data transfer from your AWS origin into CloudFront is free, and (2) CloudFront's per-GB egress rates undercut raw S3 egress at volume. So fronting a public S3 bucket with CloudFront both speeds it up and cuts the bill. The classic mistake is paying full S3 egress to the whole world instead of putting a CDN in front.

Price Classes — the one cost dial

You can restrict which edge locations serve your content:

  • Price Class All — every edge worldwide. Best performance, highest cost.
  • Price Class 200 — drops the most expensive edges (South America, Australia, NZ).
  • Price Class 100 — US, Canada, Europe & Israel only. Cheapest; users elsewhere are routed to those edges (a bit slower).
💰 Combined feel: a small site serving 500 GB/mo and 5M requests from Europe sits comfortably inside the free tier → ~$0/month. A busy media site pushing 50 TB/mo lands around $3,500–$4,000/month in transfer — at which point Price Class tuning and high-volume tiers genuinely move the needle. eu-west-1 viewers are served from Europe edges at the same ~$0.085/GB tier as the US.
🏢 Dublin Industry — How CloudFront Is Actually Used

Ryanair fronts "MyRyanair" with CloudFront to cut latency across Europe

Ryanair (HQ in Dublin) runs Europe's busiest airline website. Their customer portal MyRyanair sits behind CloudFront so that booking pages, static assets, and images load fast whether the user is in Lisbon, Warsaw, or Dublin — without hammering their origin during the daily booking rush and seasonal spikes.

Static + dynamic split

Images, CSS, JS, and fare-calendar assets are cached at the edge (long TTLs). Personalised/booking calls pass through to the origin (ALB → app tier) with short or no caching — the same distribution handles both via cache behaviours per path pattern.

Security stack

The standard pattern: CloudFront + AWS WAF (bot & rate-rule defence on the login/booking endpoints) + Shield for DDoS. The S3 asset buckets are locked to CloudFront with OAC — never public.

Wider Dublin pattern

Squarespace, Etsy, and Hostelworld (all with Dublin engineering) follow the same shape: ALB + ASG origins, S3 for media, and CloudFront for global static-asset delivery — letting a lean team serve a worldwide audience from a couple of regions.

Global Accelerator · Part 1

The Problem Global Accelerator Solves

CloudFront caches web pages. But what about a multiplayer game, a VoIP call, or an IoT fleet — traffic that can't be cached and isn't even HTTP?

CloudFront is brilliant for cacheable HTTP content. But a huge amount of internet traffic doesn't fit that mould.

A multiplayer game sends a constant stream of UDP packets (player positions, shots) — there's nothing to cache; every packet is unique and live. A VoIP call, an IoT sensor fleet speaking MQTT, a stock-trading feed — all non-HTTP, real-time, uncacheable. For these, a CDN does nothing.

Worse, these clients face two more problems:

  • The public internet is unpredictable. Between a player in Brazil and your server in Dublin, traffic hops across many random ISP networks. Each hop adds latency and jitter — terrible for a fast-paced game.
  • DNS-based failover is slow. If you use Route 53 to fail over to another region, clients keep hitting the dead region until their cached DNS entry (TTL) expires — which can be minutes. For a live game or trading app, minutes of downtime is unacceptable.
  • Some clients need fixed IPs. Corporate firewalls and game clients often whitelist specific IP addresses. But an ALB's IP changes over time, and you can't whitelist a DNS name.
💡 Why Global Accelerator Was Built

Global Accelerator gives you 2 fixed "anycast" IP addresses that act as a single, stable front door to your application — anywhere in the world. A user's traffic enters the nearest AWS edge location and then travels the rest of the way on AWS's private global backbone (fast, congestion-free) instead of the messy public internet — all the way to your servers.

It doesn't cache anything (there's nothing to cache). It makes any TCP/UDP traffic faster and more reliable, gives you static IPs clients can whitelist, and fails over between regions in ~30 seconds without waiting for any DNS TTL. Think of it as a smart, global on-ramp to the AWS highway.

Global Accelerator · Part 2

How Global Accelerator Works

Anycast IPs, the AWS backbone, and the listener → endpoint-group → endpoint hierarchy.

What "anycast" means (the key trick)

Normally one IP address maps to one location. Anycast lets the same IP address be announced from many AWS edge locations at once. When a user sends a packet to that IP, the internet naturally routes it to the nearest edge. From there, AWS takes over and carries the packet across its own backbone to your application — bypassing the slow public internet for most of the journey.

Both players hit the SAME 2 anycast IPs, enter at their nearest edge, then ride the AWS backbone to Dublin. Minimal public-internet exposure = low latency + low jitter.

The configuration hierarchy

  • Accelerator — the top-level resource. Gives you 2 static anycast IPs (from the AWS pool, or bring your own).
  • Listener — listens on a port/protocol (TCP or UDP).
  • Endpoint Group — one per AWS Region you deploy in. Has a traffic dial (send X% of traffic to this region) for gradual shifts.
  • Endpoint — the actual resource traffic lands on, with a weight for fine control.
🔑 What can be an endpoint?

Global Accelerator routes to: Application Load Balancer, Network Load Balancer, EC2 instances, and Elastic IPs. It does NOT support S3 buckets or CloudFront distributions as endpoints. Health checks continuously monitor endpoints; if one region goes unhealthy, GA reroutes to a healthy region in ~30 seconds — no DNS involved.

Global Accelerator · Part 3

When You Reach for Global Accelerator

The handful of scenarios where GA is the right (and only) answer.

Use case 1

Non-HTTP / real-time

  • Multiplayer gaming (UDP)
  • VoIP / SIP calls
  • IoT (MQTT), trading feeds
  • Anything CloudFront can't cache
Use case 2

Static IP whitelisting

  • Clients/firewalls whitelist 2 fixed IPs
  • ALB IPs change — can't whitelist
  • Enterprise B2B integrations
Use case 3

Fast regional failover

  • ~30s failover, no DNS TTL wait
  • Active-active multi-region apps
  • Deterministic, instant rerouting
Use case 4

Global TCP/UDP performance

  • Speed up any app for distant users
  • Skip the flaky public internet
  • Lower latency & jitter via backbone
⚠️ Traps

GA does NOT cache — if a question says "cache static content globally," that's CloudFront, not GA. GA does NOT front S3 or CloudFront — those aren't valid endpoints. And GA is not a cost-saver: it adds a per-GB premium, so "reduce data transfer cost" is never a reason to choose it.

Global Accelerator · Part 4

💰 What Global Accelerator Actually Costs

A fixed monthly floor plus a performance premium on every GB — the opposite shape from CloudFront.

What you're billed on

  • Fixed fee: $0.025 per accelerator per hour ≈ $18/month — charged even at zero traffic (you're reserving 2 static IPs + backbone routing).
  • Data-Transfer-Premium (DT-Premium): a per-GB charge added on top of normal AWS data transfer, based on the busier of source/destination — roughly $0.015/GB (US/Europe) up to $0.10+/GB (Asia, South America).
💰 Cost framing for the architect

CloudFront lowers your effective egress (free origin fetch + caching). Global Accelerator raises it (a premium on every GB, plus a fixed floor). So you choose GA for capability — non-HTTP, static IPs, instant failover — never to save money. A single accelerator handling 1 TB/month of US/EU traffic ≈ $18 fixed + ~$15 premium ≈ $33/month on top of normal transfer. eu-west-1 endpoints sit at the low ~$0.015/GB premium tier.

🔑 One-line memory hook

CloudFront caches HTTP. Global Accelerator races TCP/UDP. If the words "cache," "static content," or "HTTP/website" appear → CloudFront. If "static IP," "UDP/gaming/VoIP," "non-HTTP," or "fast failover without DNS" appear → Global Accelerator. Both ride AWS's edge network and both get free Shield Standard.

🤝 They can work together

They're not rivals. A global app might use CloudFront for its cacheable web/media front-end and Global Accelerator in front of NLBs for its real-time API/game traffic — the right tool for each kind of traffic.

🏢 Industry — How Global Accelerator Is Actually Used

Low-latency, static-IP global routing for gaming & real-time platforms

Riot Games (League of Legends, with a Dublin EU operations presence) is the archetypal Global Accelerator user: fast-paced multiplayer needs UDP packets to reach regional game servers with the lowest possible latency and jitter. Routing players onto the AWS backbone at the nearest edge — instead of letting UDP wander the public internet — is exactly what GA delivers, with deterministic regional failover if a game cluster goes down.

Static IPs for whitelisting

Enterprise and fintech firms in Dublin expose B2B APIs behind GA's 2 fixed anycast IPs so partner firewalls can whitelist them — something impossible with an ALB's rotating IPs. The app tier can be re-deployed or moved between regions without ever changing the public IP partners depend on.

Active-active DR

A common pattern: GA fronts NLBs in two regions (e.g. eu-west-1 Dublin + eu-central-1 Frankfurt). Traffic dials split load; health checks fail traffic over in ~30 seconds — far faster than Route 53's DNS-TTL-bound failover — for trading and payment systems that can't tolerate minutes of downtime.
